Preface

Welcome to the course book for SLAT7855 Quantitative Research Methods in Applied Linguistics, offered by the School of Languages and Cultures at The University of Queensland. This book serves as the script for the course: it contains the conceptual explanations, worked examples, and R code that we work through across the semester, and it remains freely available so that you can return to it at any time — during the course and long after it has ended.

About the Course

Course Description

This course introduces the statistical techniques used in quantitative research in applied linguistics and the language sciences more broadly. It covers both the conceptual foundations of quantitative reasoning and the practical implementation of statistical analyses using R and RStudio. The rationale and assumptions underlying the various techniques are presented alongside hands-on practice in applying them to real language data.

Assumed background: None. The course is designed for students with no prior experience in statistics, mathematics beyond school level, or programming. We start from zero and build up step by step.

For all semester-specific information — including assessment tasks, weightings, due dates, timetables, and course policies — please consult the current Electronic Course Profile (ECP) and the course Blackboard site. Assessment details change from semester to semester and are therefore deliberately not included in this book.

Rationale

Quantitative methods have become central to research in applied linguistics, second language acquisition, and linguistics. Whether a study investigates how learners acquire a second language, how linguistic features vary across speakers and contexts, or how effective a teaching intervention is, its claims typically rest on quantitative evidence. Being able to understand, evaluate, and produce such evidence is therefore no longer a specialist skill — it is a core component of research literacy in the language sciences.

At the same time, statistical methodology in language research is broad and operates at different levels of sophistication. This course does not attempt to cover everything. Instead, it follows a deliberate arc: it builds a solid conceptual foundation, introduces R as a versatile analytical tool, and then works systematically towards regression modeling — including mixed-effects models — because regression-based approaches are the workhorses of modern quantitative language research. The course closes with an outlook on tree-based methods and machine learning, connecting the material to current developments in the field.

Throughout, the emphasis is on understanding rather than recipe-following: the aim is that you know not only how to run an analysis, but why it is appropriate, what its assumptions are, and how to interpret and report its results responsibly.

Course Aims

The course has three main aims. It will provide

  1. a grounding in the basic logic and scope of quantitative reasoning (statistics) in language research,
  2. exposure to the statistical techniques commonly used in applied linguistics and related fields, and
  3. in-depth practical experience in performing, interpreting, and reporting statistical analyses using R and RStudio.

Learning Outcomes

After successfully completing this course, you should be able to:

  1. understand and apply basic statistical tests used in applied linguistics and second language acquisition research,
  2. understand the role of quantitative evidence in describing and explaining language learning and use,
  3. read quantitative research in these areas in a more critical and productive manner,
  4. import, process, analyse, and visualise data using R and RStudio, and
  5. report statistical results for a dissertation or publication in accordance with the standards of the field.

What You Will Get Out of This Course

Beyond the formal learning outcomes, the course is designed to leave you with skills that remain useful well past the end of semester:

  • Genuine programming ability. You will learn to work with R and RStudio properly — writing, understanding, and debugging code — rather than relying on point-and-click software. Programming and data analysis skills are concrete, transferable, and in demand in academia, industry, and the public sector alike.
  • The full analysis pipeline. You will learn to take a raw, messy, real-world dataset and carry it through processing, summarisation, visualisation, modeling, and reporting — the complete workflow of a quantitative study.
  • Critical research literacy. You will be able to engage with the results sections of published studies critically: to assess whether an analysis is appropriate, whether its assumptions are met, and whether its conclusions are warranted.
  • Foundations for your own research. For students planning a quantitative MA thesis or doctoral research, the course provides the methodological foundation that such projects require.
  • Reproducible research practice. Working with code means your analyses are transparent and reproducible — an increasingly important standard across the research community.

Who This Course Is For

The course focuses on quantitative methodology used in language research in applied linguistics and related fields, but it will be of interest to anyone who wants a better understanding of quantitative methods in the language sciences — including students preparing for thesis work, practising language teachers who want to engage with (or conduct) research, and anyone seeking to add solid data-analytic skills to their professional profile.

How This Book Is Organised

The book follows the arc of the course. It begins with the conceptual foundations of quantitative research (research designs, variables, hypotheses, probability, and the logic of statistical inference), then introduces R and RStudio from scratch. On that basis, it covers descriptive statistics and data visualisation before turning to inferential statistics. The second half of the book is devoted to regression modeling — simple and multiple linear regression, model diagnostics, logistic and other types of regression, and mixed-effects models — and concludes with tree-based methods and an outlook on machine learning.

Each chapter combines conceptual explanation with fully worked examples in R. All code shown in the book can be copied, run, and adapted; the datasets used are drawn from linguistics and applied linguistics.

The mapping of chapters onto teaching weeks varies slightly from semester to semester — the current schedule is available via Blackboard and the ECP.

A Note on Generative AI

Generative AI tools can be useful aids when learning R and statistics — for instance, for clarifying concepts or understanding error messages. However, the aim of this course is that you develop a genuine, independent understanding of quantitative methods: AI tools can support your learning, but they cannot replace it, and assessments in this course are designed accordingly. Any use of AI in assessed work must be acknowledged in line with UQ policy; details are provided in the ECP.
